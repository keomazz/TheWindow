Chapter One — Foundation
How Markets Actually Work

Before you look at a single chart or learn a single concept, you need to understand the true nature of financial markets. Most traders fail because they operate on a false belief about what the market is and why it moves. This chapter corrects that.

What Is a Market?

A market is a mechanism for matching buyers and sellers. Every transaction has someone on each side. Price moves because of an imbalance between the two sides — more aggressive buyers push price up, more aggressive sellers push it down. But the market is not random. At the institutional level, it is carefully managed to achieve one goal: fill large orders at the most efficient price possible.

Who Is Actually Moving the Market?

There are two categories of participants: retail traders and institutional participants (banks, hedge funds, prop firms, market makers, central banks). The difference is not just size — it is intent and information.

Retail Traders
The Crowd

Retail traders trade based on indicators, patterns, and news. They place stops at obvious levels — below support, above resistance. They buy breakouts and sell breakdowns. Because millions of retail traders learned the same textbook rules, their orders cluster at predictable locations. These clusters are the liquidity that institutions exploit.

Institutional Participants
The Engine

Institutions need to move enormous positions. You cannot buy $500 million worth of NQ at a single price — you would move the market against yourself. So institutions engineer the conditions they need: they push price into areas where enough opposite-side orders exist to fill their position. They create false moves, collect retail stops, and then move in the direction they always intended.

"The market doesn't move randomly. It moves to collect orders. Once you see this, you cannot unsee it."

Chapter Two — Foundation
Market Structure — Reading the Story

Every chart is telling a story. Market structure is the language of that story. Before trading anything, you must read price structure fluently — understanding when a trend continues, when it weakens, and when it reverses.

Swing Highs and Swing Lows

The foundation of structure analysis is swing points — the peaks and valleys where price reversed direction. They are not random. They are the footprints of institutional order flow.

Swing High
A Peak

A candle (or group) with a higher high than those on both sides. Where selling overcame buying and price reversed down. Marks the upper boundary of a range and the location of buy-side liquidity — shorts' stops and breakout buyers' pending orders.

Swing Low
A Valley

A candle (or group) with a lower low than those on both sides. Where buying overcame selling and price reversed up. Marks the lower boundary of a range and the location of sell-side liquidity — longs' stops and breakdown sellers' pending orders.

Bullish vs Bearish Structure

Bullish structure: Higher Highs (HH) and Higher Lows (HL). Each peak is higher than the last; each pullback holds above the previous low. The trend is up — only look for longs.

Bearish structure: Lower Highs (LH) and Lower Lows (LL). Each rally fails lower than the last; each drop goes further down. The trend is down — only look for shorts.

Break of Structure (BOS)

Break of Structure
BOS — Standard ICT / SMC Terminology

A BOS occurs when price moves through and closes beyond a significant swing point in the direction of the current trend, confirming continuation. Bullish BOS: price closes above the most recent swing high. Bearish BOS: price closes below the most recent swing low. In this system, a BOS on the 5M/1M chart after The Flush is one of our primary entry confirmation signals — it tells us the sweep is complete and the real directional move has begun.

Change of Character (CHOCH)

Change of Character
CHOCH — Standard ICT / SMC Terminology

A CHOCH is the first break of structure against the current trend — the initial signal that the market may be reversing. In a bullish trend: price breaks below the most recent swing low for the first time. In a bearish trend: price breaks above the most recent swing high for the first time. A CHOCH alone does not confirm reversal, but combined with The Flush and our other signals, a CHOCH on the LTF is a high-quality entry signal.

BOS vs CHOCH in practice: After The Flush at the NY open, we are watching the 5M chart for either a BOS (in a trend continuation context) or a CHOCH (in a reversal context) in the direction of The Current. Either one signals that the flush is done and the real move is beginning. The inversion FVG gives us the precise entry level after this signal.

Internal vs External Structure

External Structure
The Major Swing Points

The most significant, obvious swing highs and lows on a given timeframe — the major turning points visible when zoomed out. A break of external structure is a highly significant event marking a major directional shift.

Internal Structure
The Minor Swing Points

Smaller, shorter-term swing points that form within a larger external swing. A market can be in an external bullish trend while internally retracing with minor bearish structure. Our entry signals come from breaks of internal structure on the LTF — these are the precise moments we target.

Structure Rules
  • Always determine structure on Weekly, Daily, and 4H before opening a 5M chart
  • Only take longs in bullish HTF structure. Only take shorts in bearish HTF structure.
  • A BOS on the LTF in the direction of the HTF trend is a valid entry signal — as part of the Full Stack
  • The more timeframes that agree on structure, the stronger the setup

Chapter Three — Foundation
The Liquidity Engine

Liquidity is the heartbeat of this entire strategy. Not support and resistance. Not patterns. Liquidity. Once you understand how and why the market hunts it, every chart will tell you a completely different story.

What Is Liquidity in Trading?

Liquidity refers to clusters of pending orders sitting in the market at specific price levels. These exist because retail traders place their stops and pending entries at predictable, obvious levels. If a million retail traders all learned "put your stop below support," then a massive concentration of orders clusters at that exact level. That clustering is the liquidity — and the market moves to collect it.

Buy-Side & Sell-Side Liquidity

Buy-Side Liquidity (BSL)
Liquidity Above Highs

Located above swing highs, equal highs, and session highs. Contains: stop-losses from short sellers + buy-stop orders from breakout traders. When swept: the institution fills its sell position using the surge of buy orders. Price then drops sharply. BSL sweep = look for shorts.

Sell-Side Liquidity (SSL)
Liquidity Below Lows

Located below swing lows, equal lows, and session lows. Contains: stop-losses from long holders + sell-stop orders from breakdown traders. When swept: institution fills its buy position using the surge of sell orders. Price then rallies sharply. SSL sweep = look for longs.

The Flush — The Central Event

The Flush
Unique to This System — Also: Liquidity Sweep / Stop Hunt / Liquidity Raid

The Flush is the engineered move where price spikes through a key liquidity level — sweeping all the stops and pending orders resting there — and then closes back on the other side within one to three candles. The wick that penetrated the level is the flush. It represents the moment when all orders at that level were collected, the institutional position was filled, and price is now free to move in the real intended direction. The Flush is the central event of the entire strategy. Everything before it is preparation. Everything after it is execution.

What Makes a Flush Valid?

  • The level swept must be a significant liquidity pool — not a random minor candle wick
  • Price must close back on the other side within 1-3 candles — a slow drift through is a breakout, not a flush
  • The flush direction must be against The Current temporarily — collecting liquidity before reversing with the trend
  • A BOS or CHOCH in the direction of The Current must follow the flush on the LTF

Liquidity Pool Tiers

Tier 1 — Highest Priority
Previous Day High & Low (PDH / PDL)

The most universally watched levels in indices. Every algorithm, every institution, every retail trader tracks PDH and PDL. The concentration of orders at these levels is enormous. A Flush of PDH or PDL at the NY open is one of the cleanest, most reliable setups in the strategy. Mark these without exception every single morning.

Tier 1 — Highest Priority
London Session High & Low

The high and low created during the 3:00 AM–8:30 AM London session. The NY open at 9:30 very frequently runs directly to sweep the London high or low before making the real directional move. This is one of the most consistent patterns in indices trading. When you see NY open moving straight toward the London extreme — that is The Flush setting up.

Tier 1 — Highest Priority
Foundation Range High & Low (Asia)

The Asia session (midnight–6:00 AM EST) creates The Foundation Range. Hours of quiet, low-volume trading deposit layer upon layer of stop-losses at the Asia high and low. By the time NY opens, these levels can contain significant institutional interest. Often the NY open sweeps the Foundation Range AND the London low/high simultaneously — creating a double-confluence Flush that is extremely high quality.

Tier 2 — High Priority
Equal Highs & Equal Lows

Two or more price touches at approximately the same level. Retail traders see "double top/bottom" and expect reversal. What is actually happening: each touch deposits another layer of stops directly above or below. The market will almost always sweep these equal levels before moving directionally. Mark all equal highs and lows on your charts — they are Flush magnets.

Tier 2 — High Priority
Previous Week High & Low

Five days of accumulated orders at the weekly extremes. A Flush of the weekly high or low is a significant event that often precedes multi-day directional moves. These carry more institutional weight than daily levels.

The Magnetic Target — Draw on Liquidity

The Magnetic Target
Unique to This System — Also: Draw on Liquidity (DOL) — ICT Terminology

Price doesn't just move — it moves toward something. The Magnetic Target is the nearest, most obvious pool of liquidity in the direction of your trade. The market is literally pulled toward it — it cannot ignore a large nearby pool of orders. Before entering any trade, you must identify The Magnetic Target clearly and be able to point to it on the chart with full conviction. It is your primary take-profit level. Without a clear Magnetic Target, there is no trade.

Common mistake: Trying to trade every flush you see. Only trade flushes that (1) align with The Current, (2) occur at a Tier 1 or Tier 2 level, and (3) have a clear Magnetic Target. Anything less is noise.

Chapter Four — Foundation
ICT Concepts — The Building Blocks

These are the precise technical tools for identifying entry zones, understanding price delivery, and refining execution. Learn each one thoroughly — they form the vocabulary of everything that follows.

Fair Value Gap (FVG)

Fair Value Gap
FVG — Standard ICT Terminology. Also: Imbalance, Inefficiency

A three-candle formation where the middle candle moves so aggressively that a gap is created between the high of candle 1 and the low of candle 3 (bullish FVG), or between the low of candle 1 and the high of candle 3 (bearish FVG). This gap is an area of price that was never traded — an imbalance. The market tends to return to these areas to fill the imbalance before continuing in the original direction. FVGs on higher timeframes (4H, Daily) are premium entry zones when price returns to them.

Inversion FVG — The Primary Entry Zone

Inversion FVG
Standard ICT Terminology — A Primary Entry Signal in This System

An Inversion FVG forms when a previously bullish FVG gets violated to the downside, and then price returns to that same area — which now acts as resistance. Or a previously bearish FVG gets violated upward, and the area becomes support. After The Flush, we hunt for inversion FVGs on the 5M and 1M charts as our entry zone. The inversion FVG is the area where we place our limit orders — it is where the structural shift is confirmed at a precise price level.

Order Blocks (OB)

Order Block
OB — Standard ICT Terminology

The last candle moving in the opposite direction before a significant impulse move in the other direction. A bullish Order Block is the last bearish candle before a strong bullish impulse — institutions placed buy orders there. A bearish Order Block is the last bullish candle before a strong bearish impulse — institutions placed sell orders there. When price returns to these levels, those orders are still resting, creating a high-probability reaction zone. An OB that also contains an FVG is an especially high-quality level.

Premium and Discount

Between any two significant swing points, there is a midpoint — the 50% level. Premium is above the midpoint (expensive). Discount is below it (cheap).

Discount Zone
Buy Here (Bullish Context)

In a bullish setup, enter longs in discount — price is cheap relative to the range and likely to go up. Entering in premium on a long is chasing. Poor execution.

Premium Zone
Sell Here (Bearish Context)

In a bearish setup, enter shorts in premium — price is expensive and likely to fall. Entering a short in discount is selling cheap. Poor execution.

Practical application: When your inversion FVG or OB sits in the correct zone (discount for longs, premium for shorts), the entry quality is significantly higher. This is a filter for the best setups.

NWOG — New Week Opening Gap

New Week Opening Gap
NWOG — Standard ICT Terminology

When futures open Sunday evening at 6:00 PM EST, there is frequently a gap between Friday's close and Sunday's open. This NWOG acts as a powerful weekly Magnetic Target — the market has a very strong tendency to fill it at some point during the week. Always mark the NWOG on your weekly chart early in the week. A gap above is a bullish draw; a gap below is a bearish draw.

Optimal Trade Entry (OTE)

Optimal Trade Entry
OTE — Standard ICT Terminology

A Fibonacci-based concept identifying the ideal retracement zone after an impulse move. After The Flush creates a strong impulse, price often retraces. The OTE zone is the 61.8%–79% Fibonacci retracement of that impulse. Institutions re-enter their positions here — "reloading" at a better price. When your inversion FVG sits within the OTE zone, the entry is extremely high quality. On TradingView: draw Fibonacci retracement from the flush low to the first impulse high (for longs). The 61.8–79% zone is your OTE entry area.

The HTF Map — Top-Down Analysis

The Current is your directional bias — the higher timeframe flow everything is built upon. Getting this right is 80% of the work. A perfect entry in the wrong direction is still a losing trade.

The Current

The Current
Unique to This System — Also: HTF Bias, Directional Bias

The directional flow of the market on higher timeframes — the force everything else is moving with or against. Just as you swim with a river's current rather than fighting it, you trade with The Current, never against it. A bullish Current means HTF structure is making higher highs and higher lows, the major draw is on buy-side liquidity above, and you are exclusively looking for longs. Determining The Current accurately every morning is the single most important step in your preparation.

The Top-Down Analysis Flow

Always analyze from the highest timeframe down. Never start with a 5-minute chart and then justify a trade on the higher timeframes.

The Read — Morning Preparation Ritual

The Read
Unique to This System — Pre-Market Analysis Ritual

The Read is the 15–20 minute morning preparation session you complete every trading day before 9:00 AM. It is the act of marking all key levels, determining The Current, and identifying the Magnetic Target for the day. Without The Read, you are entering The Window blind. The Read is not optional — it is the foundation of every trade you will take.

The Read Checklist — Complete Every Morning Before 9:00 AM
  • Weekly High and Low (current week)
  • Previous Week High and Low
  • Previous Day High (PDH) and Previous Day Low (PDL)
  • Active Fair Value Gaps on Daily and 4H
  • Key Order Blocks on Daily and 4H
  • Foundation Range High and Low (Asia session: midnight–6:00 AM EST)
  • London Session High and Low (3:00 AM–8:30 AM EST)
  • NWOG if early in the week
  • Major round/psychological numbers nearby
  • The Current: write down Bullish or Bearish and why
  • The Magnetic Target: write down the specific level and why

Determining The Current — Step by Step

2

Is daily structure aligned with weekly? Strong confirmation if yes. If they disagree, wait for clarity or do not trade.
3

The most obvious liquidity pool in the direction of the daily trend. PDH/PDL? Daily FVG? Weekly high/low? This is where the market is drawing toward today.

Is 4H structure aligned? If the 4H is in a retracement against the daily trend, you may need to wait for it to complete before the setup forms.

Did London trend with The Current or create a counter-trend move? A London move against The Current is a prime setup for NY to flush it and continue the original direction.

Write in your journal: "The Current is BULLISH / BEARISH. My Magnetic Target is [level]. Looking for longs / shorts." Written commitment stops mid-session flip-flopping.
When The Current is unclear: The answer is simple — you do not trade. A day with no clear Current is Dead Water. Sit on your hands. Protect your capital on uncertain days. That is a skill, not a weakness.

Chapter Six — Methodology
Asia & London Sessions — Reading the Setup

The Asia and London sessions are not just time periods — they are the architectural foundation upon which the NY open trade is built every single day. Understanding what these sessions do and why gives you a massive edge before the NY bell even rings.

The Foundation Range — Asia Session

The Foundation Range
Unique to This System — Also: Asia Range, Midnight Range, Killzone Range

The price range created during the Asia session — roughly midnight to 6:00 AM EST. This session is characterized by low volume, narrow price movement, and accumulation. The market is resting. But in that rest, every hour of ranging deposits stop-losses at the session high and low. Retail traders who trade Asia place their stops just outside the range. By the time London and NY arrive, the Foundation Range high and low are packed with orders — making them prime Flush targets. Mark the Foundation Range high and low every single morning without exception.

Why It's Called The Foundation

Think architecturally: Asia sets the foundation. London and NY build on top of it. Without understanding what Asia built, you're trying to understand a building without seeing its base. The Foundation Range high and low are the first levels you mark after midnight. They give the initial framework for where the first flushes of the day might occur.

The London Session

London (3:00 AM–8:30 AM EST) is the first major liquidity event of the day. It does one of two things:

Scenario A
London Continues The Current

London moves in the direction of the daily bias, creating a clean directional move and building up opposite-side liquidity. Bullish Current: London rallies, creating sell-side liquidity below the London low. NY sweeps the London low (The Flush) and continues up.

Scenario B
London Creates a False Move

London moves against The Current — drawing in retail traders betting on a reversal. NY then flushes the London extreme and continues in The Current's direction. This is actually the more common and profitable scenario for NY traders. The false London move sets up the NY trade perfectly.

The Classic NY Open Pattern

1
London creates a clear high and low
These levels are now packed with liquidity. The stage is set.
2
9:30 — NY opens with a spike
Price makes an immediate aggressive move — often against the current intraday direction — heading toward a key liquidity level.
3
The Flush — London level is taken
Price spikes through the London high or low, sweeps all stops, creates a wick, and then immediately reverses. The institutional fill happens here.
4
BOS on the 5M chart
Price breaks a minor swing point in the direction of The Current — confirming the flush is complete and the real move is beginning.
5
Entry at inversion FVG
Price may pull back slightly to the inversion FVG zone at The Flush area. This is the entry. Tight stop. Magnetic Target set.
6
Move to the Magnetic Target
The real directional move begins. Price moves toward the daily Magnetic Target — PDH/PDL, weekly level, or daily FVG. Take profits.
Important: Keep your charts open from 9:00 AM — not just 9:30 AM. The flush can begin building in the pre-market minutes before the official bell. Being there at 9:00 AM means you're ready. Being there at 9:30 AM means you might already be late.

Chapter Seven — Entry Execution
The NY Open — Entry Execution

This is the chapter everything builds toward. Nine chapters of preparation for one hour of execution. Every concept — liquidity, structure, FVGs, sessions — was preparing you for this moment. Here is the complete step-by-step execution of the strategy, from the first candle of the NY open all the way to the moment you close your trade.

The Core Setup In One Sentence

The Setup
The complete sequence — memorize this

Identify your HTF bias and daily liquidity target → wait for the NY open to sweep a key liquidity level → drop to 5M and confirm a Break of Structure → drop to 2M or 1M and find the inversion FVG → enter at the inversion FVG with stop beyond the sweep wick → target the next major liquidity pool. That's it. Everything in this chapter is the detailed explanation of each of those steps.

The Four Conditions — All Must Be Present

Condition 1
Clear HTF Directional Bias

Before 9:30 AM, you must know whether you are looking for longs or shorts. Daily and 4H structure must both be clearly bullish or bearish. You must be able to write one sentence in your journal: "Today I am looking for longs / shorts because the daily and 4H are both making higher highs and higher lows / lower highs and lower lows." If you cannot write that sentence clearly, you do not trade today.

Condition 2
Liquidity Sweep at a Key Level

A spike through a significant liquidity level — London high or low, Asia range high or low, PDH/PDL, equal highs or lows, or weekly high/low — that closes back on the other side within 1-3 candles. The sweep must be in the opposite direction of your HTF bias temporarily. A bullish bias needs a sell-side sweep (price spikes below a low). A bearish bias needs a buy-side sweep (price spikes above a high). If the sweep is in the same direction as your bias without sweeping opposite-side liquidity first, wait.

Condition 3
BOS on 5M + Inversion FVG on 2M/1M

After the sweep: a Break of Structure on the 5M chart in the direction of your bias (price closes above the last 5M swing high for longs, or below the last 5M swing low for shorts). Then drop to the 2M or 1M chart and find the inversion FVG that formed in the area of the sweep. This is your entry zone. Both the BOS and the inversion FVG must be present — the BOS tells you the direction changed, the inversion FVG tells you exactly where to enter.

Condition 4
Clear Draw on Liquidity (Your Target)

You must identify your take-profit target before you enter the trade. Not after — before. Look at the next obvious pool of liquidity in the direction of your trade: the nearest PDH or PDL you haven't hit yet, equal highs or lows in your direction, the previous week high or low, or an unfilled daily FVG above or below. If there is no clear, obvious target nearby, the trade has no destination — and you don't take it.

SMT Divergence — The Premium Confirmation

SMT Divergence
Smart Money Technique — ICT Terminology. The premium signal that upgrades any setup.

SMT divergence happens when NQ (NASDAQ futures) and ES (S&P 500 futures) fail to confirm each other's move at a key moment. These two instruments are correlated — they should move together. When they diverge, it reveals that one instrument's move is not genuine. Specifically: if NQ makes a new high at the sweep but ES does not make a corresponding new high, the NQ high is fake. The institutional selling is already happening in ES while NQ is still being pushed up to collect buy-stops. The divergence is the signal. When SMT is present alongside a sweep and BOS, you have the highest possible quality setup — enter with full confidence and maximum position size.

How to Use SMT Divergence on TradingView

  • Open TradingView and use the "Compare" function — add NQ to the ES chart (or vice versa) so both plot on the same chart
  • Alternatively, use the split-screen layout: NQ on the left panel, ES on the right panel, both on the 5M timeframe
  • At 9:30 AM when the sweep happens, look at both simultaneously: did NQ make a new high/low that ES did not match?
  • The divergence must be visually obvious — not a few ticks difference, but a clear and significant separation between the two instruments at the same moment
  • Bearish SMT: NQ makes a new high (sweeping buy-side), ES does not → short signal
  • Bullish SMT: NQ makes a new low (sweeping sell-side), ES does not → long signal
  • You can also use YM (Dow Jones) as a third reference instrument for additional confirmation

The Timeframe Drill-Down — From 5M to 1M

Most traders make the mistake of trying to find their entry on the 5M chart. The 5M gives you the structural confirmation (BOS), but the actual entry precision comes from going lower — to the 2M and 1M charts. Here is why this matters, and exactly how to do it.

Step 1 — 5M Chart
Confirm the BOS on 5M

After the sweep, stay on the 5M chart and wait for price to break structure. For a long: wait for a 5M candle to close above the most recent 5M swing high. For a short: wait for a 5M candle to close below the most recent 5M swing low. This BOS tells you the direction of the sweep has ended and the reversal has begun. Do NOT enter here — this is confirmation, not the entry. The entry comes on a lower timeframe.

Step 2 — 2M Chart
Find the Inversion FVG on 2M

On the 2M chart, the sweep candle and the first few recovery candles will have created imbalances. Find the FVG that now represents the inversion zone — this is an area where the market was moving so fast in one direction that price never traded there before reversing. When price retraces into this zone, that is the entry. The 2M inversion FVG is typically the best balance between precision and reliability.

Stop-Loss Placement — Exact Rules

The stop-loss in this strategy is not placed at a "comfortable" distance or at a round number. It is placed at a specific, logical level that tells you definitively that the setup has failed. Here is exactly where it goes:

Long Trade Stop-Loss
Below the Sweep Wick Low

Your stop goes 2-5 ticks below the absolute lowest wick of the sell-side sweep candle on the timeframe you traded. If the sweep wick on the 1M chart went to 21,340 — your stop is at 21,335 or 21,336. Not at 21,320. Not at "below this support zone." Exactly below the wick with a small buffer. The logic: if price goes back below that wick, the sweep failed, the setup is invalid, and you need to be out immediately. The tighter the stop, the better the R:R — but you must give it enough room that normal wick noise doesn't stop you out.

Short Trade Stop-Loss
Above the Sweep Wick High

Your stop goes 2-5 ticks above the absolute highest wick of the buy-side sweep candle. If the sweep wick went to 21,580 — your stop is at 21,583 or 21,585. Not at "above resistance." Exactly above the wick with a small buffer. If price goes back above that wick, the buy-side sweep failed to create a reversal — the market is actually breaking out upward and your short thesis is wrong. Exit immediately at a small loss before it becomes a large one.

The most common stop-loss mistake: Placing the stop too far away because you are "afraid of being stopped out." A wider stop means a worse R:R, which means you need to win more often to be profitable. The sweep wick is not chosen arbitrarily — it is the exact level where the institutional order was placed. If the market goes back through that level, the institution changed its mind, and you should too.

The Inversion FVG — Deep Explanation

Because the inversion FVG is the most precise and important entry signal in this strategy, it deserves a full dedicated explanation. Most traders who struggle with entries are struggling here — they don't identify the inversion FVG correctly, or they don't wait for price to retrace into it.

What Exactly Is an Inversion FVG?

During the sweep candle, the market moves so aggressively in one direction that it creates a Fair Value Gap — a zone between candle 1's wick and candle 3's wick that was never traded. Then the BOS happens and price reverses. As price reverses, it will often retrace back into this FVG zone. But now, the polarity of the FVG has flipped — what was a bearish FVG (created by a downward sweep) is now acting as support on the retracement. That is the inversion. Price is "testing" the area where the sweep happened, confirming that the institutional fill at that level is genuine, and then continuing in the new direction.

How to Identify It on the 2M Chart

Identifying the Inversion FVG — Step by Step
  • Switch to the 2M chart immediately after the 5M BOS is confirmed
  • Find the sweep candle — the one that created the wick beyond the key level. Note its body and wicks.
  • Look at the candle immediately before and immediately after the sweep candle. There should be a gap between the wick of the pre-sweep candle and the wick of the post-sweep candle. That gap is the FVG.
  • For a bullish setup (sell-side sweep): the FVG is below the BOS level. It will be between the high wick of the candle before the sweep and the low wick of the candle after the sweep recovery.
  • For a bearish setup (buy-side sweep): the FVG is above the BOS level. It sits in the area where the sweep wick extended to before price reversed down.
  • Draw a rectangle on the chart covering the full range of the FVG zone. This is your entry zone. Place your limit order at the midpoint of this rectangle, or at the 50% level of the zone.
  • If the inversion FVG also aligns with the Fibonacci OTE zone (61.8–79% retracement of the initial recovery move), the entry is of exceptional quality.

When the Inversion FVG Doesn't Appear

Sometimes the market moves so fast after the sweep that no significant FVG forms on the 2M chart, or price never retraces into the FVG zone. In this case you have two options:

  • Option A — Skip the trade: If the inversion FVG is not visible or price moves straight away without retracing, the entry opportunity has passed. Do not chase. Wait for the next setup. This is the cleanest decision — the setup was incomplete.
  • Option B — Enter on the 5M BOS candle close: If the BOS candle on the 5M is exceptionally strong and all other conditions are perfect (clear bias, clear sweep, clear target, SMT divergence), you can enter on the close of the BOS candle itself. Your stop is still beyond the sweep wick. Your position size should be reduced (half your normal size) because the entry is less precise.

The Math That Changes Everything

Position Sizing Formula

Calculate Before Every Single Trade
  • Step 1: Dollar risk = Account size × Risk %  (e.g. $50,000 × 1% = $500)
  • Step 2: Stop distance in points  (e.g. stop is 20 points from entry on NQ)
  • Step 3: Dollar per point per contract  (NQ = $20/pt, MNQ = $2/pt, ES = $50/pt, MES = $5/pt)
  • Step 4: Contracts = Dollar risk ÷ (Stop distance × Dollar per point)
  • Example: $500 ÷ (20 × $20) = $500 ÷ $400 = 1.25 → round down to 1 contract

Failure 1
Overtrading

Taking trades outside The Window, without a Full Stack, or out of boredom. The strategy has specific conditions. Diluting those conditions dilutes the edge. Five trades a day when only one is valid means 80% of your trades are pure noise — and you're paying commission and risk on all of them.

Failure 2
Moving Stop-Losses

The moment you move a stop further away to avoid a loss, you have broken the risk management system. What started as -1R becomes -3R, then -5R, then a margin call. A stop-loss is a decision made before emotions are involved. Honor that decision.

Failure 3
Revenge Trading

Taking an immediate second trade to "win back" a loss. Revenge trades are almost always taken in poor conditions, with too much size, and too much emotion. They turn a -1R day into a -4R day. Two consecutive losses = done for the day. Non-negotiable.

Failure 4
Not Journaling

Operating in the dark. Making the same mistakes repeatedly because there's no record of patterns. Without the journal, progress is random. With it, progress is systematic. The journal is not optional.

Failure 5
Abandoning the System During Drawdown

Changing or abandoning the strategy precisely during a temporary drawdown — exactly when you should continue executing it consistently. Every strategy has losing streaks. The strategy that worked for 3 profitable months doesn't stop working in a rough week. Trust the system. Execute the next valid Full Stack with full conviction.

A Full Stack setup that also has a confirmed Split Signal. The highest-quality setup in the system. Trade with maximum conviction and full size.
The Split Signal
Unique — Also: SMT Divergence (ICT)
Divergence between NQ and ES at a liquidity level. One instrument makes a new high/low while the other fails to confirm. Signals the move is engineered and reversal is imminent.
Dead Water
Unique to this system
A session where The Current is unclear, no valid Flush develops, or no Magnetic Target is identifiable. No trade is the correct decision on Dead Water days.
The Read
Unique to this system
The 15–20 minute pre-market preparation ritual performed every morning before 9:00 AM. Marking all key levels, determining The Current, identifying the Magnetic Target.
Fair Value Gap (FVG)
Standard ICT Terminology
A three-candle imbalance where the middle candle moves so strongly that a gap is created between the wick of candle 1 and the wick of candle 3. Price tends to return and fill these areas.
Inversion FVG
Standard ICT Terminology
An FVG that has been violated and now acts as opposite-side support/resistance. A primary entry zone in this system — forms after The Flush in the area of the sweep.
Order Block (OB)
Standard ICT Terminology
The last opposing candle before a significant impulse move. Where institutional orders were placed. High-probability support/resistance when price returns.
Break of Structure (BOS)
Standard ICT / SMC Terminology
Price moves through and closes beyond a significant swing point in the direction of the current trend. Confirms trend continuation. On LTF after The Flush, one of our primary entry confirmation signals.
Change of Character (CHOCH)
Standard ICT / SMC Terminology
The first break of structure against the current trend — the initial reversal signal. Combined with The Flush, a valid LTF entry signal.
BSL / SSL
Standard ICT Terminology
Buy-Side Liquidity (above highs) and Sell-Side Liquidity (below lows). Pools of stops and pending orders that the market engineers moves to collect.
Premium / Discount
Standard ICT Terminology
Premium: above the 50% midpoint of a price range — expensive, sell in bearish context. Discount: below 50% — cheap, buy in bullish context. Entering in the correct zone dramatically improves R:R.
NWOG
Standard ICT — New Week Opening Gap
Gap between Friday's close and Sunday's open on futures. Acts as a weekly Magnetic Target — very strong tendency to be filled during the week.
Optimal Trade Entry (OTE)
Standard ICT Terminology
The Fibonacci 61.8%–79% retracement zone of a post-flush impulse move. The highest probability retracement entry level. When your inversion FVG sits within the OTE, the entry quality is exceptional.
PDH / PDL
Standard Trading Terminology
Previous Day High and Previous Day Low. The most universally watched levels in indices. Tier 1 Flush targets at the NY open.
R:R / R Multiple
Standard Trading Terminology
Risk-to-Reward ratio. A 3:1 R:R: for every $1 risked, the target is $3 profit. +2R means you made 2× your risk. -1R means you lost your full risk amount.
